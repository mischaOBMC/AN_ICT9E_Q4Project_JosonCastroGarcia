Recipe:

Sinigang is a beloved Filipino sour soup typically made with pork, shrimp, or fish, simmered with a variety of vegetables and a tangy souring agent. Pork Sinigang is one of the most popular variations. Here are the ingredients for this comforting dish:

Main Ingredients:
 1 lb (450g) pork belly, pork ribs, or pork shoulder, cut into chunks
1 medium onion, quartered
2 medium tomatoes, quartered
2-3 long green chili peppers (optional, for a bit of heat)
1-2 tablespoons fish sauce (patis), or to taste
6 cups water (or beef/chicken broth for extra flavor)
1 packet of sinigang mix (optional, or substitute with fresh tamarind)
1-2 tablespoons tamarind paste or fresh tamarind pods (if not using sinigang mix)
1 radish (labanos), peeled and sliced
1 medium eggplant (talong), sliced into rounds
1-2 cups kangkong (water spinach) or spinach
1 cup string beans (sitaw), cut into 2-3 inch lengths
1-2 medium potatoes, peeled and sliced (optional, for added texture)
1-2 cups okra (optional)
1-2 pieces long green chili peppers (optional, for added flavor)

Procedure:

1. Prepare the Pork and Vegetables
 -In a large pot, add the pork (belly, ribs, or shoulder), onion, and tomatoes. Pour in 6 cups of water (or broth if you prefer a richer flavor).
-Bring the mixture to a boil over high heat. Once it begins to boil, lower the heat and let it simmer for about 45 minutes to 1 hour. This will allow the pork to become tender and the flavors to meld together.
2. Add the Souring Agent
 -Once the pork is tender, add the tamarind paste or the fresh tamarind pods to the pot. If you're using a sinigang mix, you can add it at this point, but fresh tamarind always gives a more authentic flavor.
-If you're using fresh tamarind pods, crush them lightly with a spoon to release the sour juice into the broth.
-Simmer for about 10-15 minutes to allow the tamarind to fully incorporate into the broth.
3. Season the Broth
 -Add fish sauce to taste, along with salt and pepper. Stir and taste the broth; adjust the sourness by adding more tamarind if needed, or more fish sauce for saltiness.
-You can also add green chili peppers at this point if you like a bit of heat.
4. Add the Vegetables
 -Add the radish, eggplant, string beans, okra, and potatoes (if using). Let these cook for about 10-15 minutes, until they are tender but still have a little bite.
-Add the kangkong (water spinach) or regular spinach in the last few minutes of cooking to preserve its freshness and vibrant color.
5. Final Adjustments
 -Taste the broth once more to ensure it has the right balance of sourness and saltiness. You can add more fish sauce or a bit more tamarind paste if needed.
-Adjust the heat level by adding more chili peppers if you want more spice.
6. Serve
 -Once everything is cooked to your liking, remove the pot from the heat.
-Serve the Sinigang na Baboy hot with a side of steamed white rice. The sour, savory broth paired with tender pork and vegetables is a comforting, flavorful meal.

Tips:

 Souring options: Aside from tamarind, you can also use green mango, calamansi, or guava to sour your sinigang, depending on your preference and whatâ€™s available.
Flavor variations: You can add lemongrass for an aromatic boost or a dash of sugar if you want to balance out the sourness.
Leftovers: Sinigang is often tastier the next day as the flavors continue to meld. Just reheat and enjoy!

Enjoy your warm, tangy, and comforting Sinigang na Baboy, a staple of Filipino cuisine!
