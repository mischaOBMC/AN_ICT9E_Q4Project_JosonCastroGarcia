Recipe:

Kare-Kare is a rich and savory Filipino stew made with oxtail, tripe, or other meats, and cooked in a creamy peanut sauce. Traditionally served with a side of bagoong (fermented shrimp paste), it's a dish full of flavor and heartiness, often enjoyed during special occasions or family gatherings.

Main Ingredients:
 1 lb (450g) oxtail, cut into pieces (or substitute with beef short ribs, tripe, or pork hock)
1/2 lb (225g) beef tripe (optional, for authentic texture)
1/2 cup peanut butter (smooth or chunky, depending on preference)
1 medium onion, quartered
3 cloves garlic, minced
2 tablespoons annatto seeds (atsuete) or 1 tablespoon annatto powder (for color)
6 cups water (or beef broth for more depth of flavor)
1 medium eggplant, sliced into rounds
1-2 banana blossoms (optional, for added flavor and texture)
1 bunch kangkong (water spinach) or bok choy
1/2 cup string beans (sitaw), cut into 2-3 inch lengths
1 tablespoon fish sauce (or salt), to taste
1 tablespoon bagoong (fermented shrimp paste), for serving

Procedure:

1. Prepare the Meat
 -In a large pot, place the oxtail and beef tripe (if using). Cover with water and bring to a boil over medium-high heat
. -Once it starts to boil, skim off any impurities (foam) that rise to the surface, then reduce the heat to low. Simmer for about 1.5 to 2 hours, or until the meat becomes tender and the collagen in the oxtail releases, creating a rich broth.
2. Prepare the Annatto Oil
 -While the meat is simmering, heat a small saucepan over medium heat and add a tablespoon of vegetable oil.
-Once the oil is hot, add the annatto seeds and cook for about 2-3 minutes until the oil turns a vibrant orange. Remove the seeds and discard them, leaving the annatto-infused oil. Set aside.
3. Make the Peanut Sauce
 -Once the oxtail is tender, add the onion and garlic to the pot with the meat and continue to simmer for about 10 minutes.
-Stir in the peanut butter, making sure it dissolves into the broth, giving the soup a creamy, peanut-flavored texture.
-Add the annatto oil to the pot for color and flavor. Stir to combine. If the stew gets too thick, you can add a bit more water or broth to reach your desired consistency.
S-eason with fish sauce (or salt) to taste. Simmer for another 5-10 minutes to let the flavors meld together.
4. Cook the Vegetables
 -In the last 10-15 minutes of cooking, add the eggplant, string beans, and banana blossoms (if using). Let them cook until tender.
-Add the kangkong (water spinach) or bok choy last, cooking for about 2-3 minutes just until the greens are wilted and tender.
5. Serve
 -Once the stew is fully cooked and the flavors have developed, remove the pot from the heat.
-Serve the Kare-Kare hot with a side of steamed white rice and a small bowl of bagoong (fermented shrimp paste) on the side for added flavor.

Tips:

Meat Variations: While oxtail is the most common choice for Kare-Kare, you can substitute or combine with other cuts of beef, pork hock, or even chicken if preferred.
Bagoong: Kare-Kare is traditionally served with a small dish of bagoong (fermented shrimp paste) for a salty contrast to the rich peanut flavor. You can either serve it on the side or mix it directly into the stew.
Peanut Butter: For a more authentic taste, you can make your own peanut paste by blending roasted peanuts. However, store-bought peanut butter works perfectly fine.
